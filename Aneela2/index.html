Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy is built upon three pillars that guide my approach as a teaching-focused academic.

üî•

Burning

Evidence-Based Pedagogy & Curriculum Design

I adopt a research-informed and scaffolded approach to teaching complex scientific concepts. Drawing on Bloom's Taxonomy and cognitive load theory, I structure learning experiences that build from foundational knowledge to higher-order thinking.

  • Design sequenced and chunked content to enhance comprehension and retention
  • Use formative assessment and timely feedback to support iterative learning
  • Integrate digital tools and simulations to make abstract chemical and biological processes tangible and engaging
üí°

Illuminating

Fostering Collaboration & Reflective Learning

I believe learning is a social and reflective process. Through peer instruction, mentorship, and cooperative problem-solving, I create a classroom culture where students teach and learn from one another.

"You are very friendly and helpful, you make sure we understand the lesson."
"I liked how you individually checked in with everyone to make sure they understood the topic."
"You encourage us to explain things so we know what we are learning."
üå±

Well-being

Inclusive, Supportive & Holistic Education

I intentionally embed well-being and inclusivity into my teaching practice. By relating scientific concepts to nutrition, mental resilience, and real-world applications, I help students see the relevance of science to their lives.

Continuous improvement is central to my practice. I actively seek and respond to student feedback to refine my teaching, simplifying visual materials and incorporating more inclusive questioning techniques based on student suggestions.

Curriculum Design & Innovation

Sample Unit Outline

Unit: Biochemistry & Genetics Course: Associate Degree in Applied Science (AD012) Institution: RMIT University Period: Semester 2, 2025

This unit introduces core principles of biochemistry and genetics with an emphasis on practical application and interdisciplinary connection. Students explore biomolecules, metabolic pathways, gene expression, and inheritance patterns through a blended learning approach that integrates theory, virtual labs, case-based learning, and collaborative projects.

Learning Outcomes

  1. LO1: Explain the structure and function of key biomolecules and their role in cellular processes.
  2. LO2: Describe fundamental genetic mechanisms, including DNA replication, transcription, translation, and inheritance.
  3. LO3: Apply theoretical knowledge to analyse biochemical and genetic data in virtual and simulated laboratory environments.
  4. LO4: Collaborate effectively in teams to research and present on a contemporary issue in biochemistry or genetics.
  5. LO5: Reflect on the ethical, social, and health-related implications of biochemical and genetic technologies.

Mapping for UDL Readiness

An Analysis of Pre-Lesson Planning Practices of Teachers at RMIT

Conducted a mixed-methods study to evaluate how VET teachers integrate Universal Design for Learning (UDL) into lesson planning. Developed and validated a UDL Readiness Self-Assessment Tool now used in professional development workshops.

Key Findings

  • 62% of teachers used UDL intentionally
  • Barriers identified: time constraints, training gaps
  • Enablers identified: resources, collaboration
02

Addressing Equity in Collaborative Work

Structured Group Roles for Laboratory Settings

Introduced structured group roles (Technician, Recorder, Calculator, Spokesperson) to promote equitable participation in laboratory settings. Investigated the impact on mixed-ability and EAL learners through reflective case studies and participation analytics.

Outcomes

  • More balanced engagement across all students
  • Reduced dominance by high-achieving students
  • Created role cards and contribution trackers

Embedding LLN in Science Classrooms

Language, Literacy & Numeracy Supports for Diverse Learners

Designed and implemented scaffolded language, literacy, and numeracy (LLN) supports for diverse learners, including EAL students. Created plain-language protocols, visual guides, and interactive drills to maintain rigor while improving accessibility.

Results

  • 85% of EAL learners reported better comprehension
  • Increased confidence without compromising safety or subject depth
  • Developed adaptable templates for modified safety sheets
04

AI and Creativity in Science Education

Presented at ASEF Higher Education Innovation Lab, December 2025

Exploring concerns that generative AI may erode student originality and critical thinking by encouraging passive content consumption. Developed and presented a structured model for "AI-mediated creativity" that integrates prompt engineering, ethical AI use, and scaffolded creative tasks within science curricula.

Framework Principles

  • Pre-AI Cognitive Priming: Mandatory independent brainstorming before AI use
  • Relationship Monitoring: Track idea diversity alongside quantity
  • Critical Transformation Protocol: "Transform, don't refine" AI outputs
  • Category Expansion Technique: Use AI to explore opposite categories
